# Purpose

Knowledge Accessibility defines how the same cultural knowledge is presented appropriately for different audiences without duplicating content or altering factual accuracy.

The Living Atlas stores knowledge once.

It presents knowledge differently depending on the user's goals, expertise, and context.

Knowledge Accessibility is a presentation strategy, not a data strategy.

---

# Vision

Knowledge should be equally accessible to everyone.

Accessibility does not mean simplifying knowledge until it loses meaning.

Accessibility means presenting knowledge at the right depth for the right audience while preserving authenticity.

The platform should support lifelong learning.

A casual reader should be able to become an enthusiast.

An enthusiast should become a researcher.

A researcher should become a contributor.

---

# Philosophy

Traditional systems expose documents.

The Living Atlas exposes understanding.

Every knowledge object should answer different questions depending on who is asking.

Example

Reader

"What happened?"

Researcher

"What evidence supports this?"

Writer

"What themes can inspire a story?"

Anthropologist

"How does this vary across regions?"

The underlying knowledge remains identical.

Only the presentation changes.

# Cultural Context

Whenever cultural concepts appear, provide context before interpretation.

Example

Instead of:

"Kuntilanak is..."

Prefer:

"In many Indonesian traditions, Kuntilanak is described as..."

Regional variation is encouraged.

Universal claims are avoided.

# Design Principles

Store knowledge once.

Present knowledge many ways.

Respect cultural diversity.

Preserve evidence.

Encourage curiosity.

Never sacrifice scholarly integrity for simplicity.

---

# Guiding Principle

Knowledge becomes truly accessible when every user can understand it without reducing its richness.

The Living Atlas adapts its presentation to the learner—not the knowledge itself.

One knowledge graph.

Many ways to understand it.

One shared cultural heritage.
